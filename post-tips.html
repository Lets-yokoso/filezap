7 Image Conversion Mistakes Everyone Makes (And How to Fix Them)

📅 May 2025⏱ 4 min read✍️ Filezap Team

Converting images should be simple. And it is — when you know what you're doing. But these seven mistakes trip up everyone from beginners to people who've been doing it for years.

1

Converting PNG to JPG and expecting the same quality

PNG is lossless — every pixel is stored perfectly. JPG is lossy — it throws away data to shrink the file. When you convert PNG → JPG, you permanently lose quality. The bigger the compression, the worse it looks.

Worse: if you then convert that JPG back to PNG, you get a lossless copy of a degraded image. The damage is already done.

✓ Fix: Set quality to 90%+ in Filezap when converting to JPG. Preview it before you download.
2

Converting a logo or icon to JPG

Logos have sharp edges, flat colors, and often a transparent background. JPG handles none of these well. You'll get ugly compression artifacts around edges and a white background where transparency used to be.

✓ Fix: Use PNG or WEBP for logos. Always. JPG is for photographs, not graphics.
3

Not checking the output before downloading

You convert a file, it downloads automatically, and only after uploading it somewhere do you notice it looks terrible. Most online tools skip the preview step entirely.

✓ Fix: Use Filezap's built-in preview. See the result first, download only if it looks right.
4

Upscaling a small image and expecting it to look sharp

You can't get detail that wasn't there. Doubling the size of a 200×200 pixel image gives you a blurry 400×400 image. Conversion doesn't add pixels — it just stretches existing ones.

✓ Fix: Only resize down, not up. For upscaling, use an AI upscaler first, then convert.
5

Ignoring the color profile when converting for print

Screens use RGB (red, green, blue light). Printers use CMYK (cyan, magenta, yellow, black ink). A vivid blue on screen might look dull or purple when printed, because the CMYK gamut can't reproduce all RGB colors.

✓ Fix: For anything going to print, convert to TIFF or PDF with a CMYK color profile. Ask your printer what they need.
6

Converting GIF to MP4 and wondering why it's huge

GIF animations are notoriously large. But converting GIF → MP4 without compression settings can produce a file 10x larger than a well-compressed version. The conversion is right, the settings are wrong.

✓ Fix: Use Filezap's quality slider when converting video. A CRF value around 23–28 gives a great size/quality balance.
7

Using a random online tool and uploading sensitive files

Many "free" online converters upload your files to their servers, process them, and may store them indefinitely. If you're converting documents with personal info, contracts, or private photos — that's a real risk.

✓ Fix: Filezap processes images directly in your browser. Nothing leaves your device for simple image conversions. Server-side conversions are deleted immediately after the session.

🏆 The Golden Rule

Always preview before downloading. If the converted file doesn't look right, adjust the settings and try again. Filezap's live preview is specifically designed to catch these problems before they become your problem.
